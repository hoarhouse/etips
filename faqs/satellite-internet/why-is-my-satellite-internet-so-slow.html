Why Is My Satellite Internet So Slow?

Short Answer:

Satellite internet slowdowns occur from multiple causes: weather interference (rain/snow absorbing signal), obstructions blocking dish view, network congestion during peak hours (5-11 PM), data cap throttling (HughesNet/Viasat throttle to 1-3 Mbps after limits), poor dish alignment, outdated equipment firmware, or too many connected devices. For emergency communications, immediately check for obstructions (Starlink app), verify data usage hasn't triggered caps, and ensure dish has clear sky view. Quick fixes can restore critical connectivity during disasters.

Provider-Specific Slowdown Causes

Different satellite providers have unique issues causing slow speeds that require specific troubleshooting approaches.

Starlink Specific Issues

  • Obstruction Interruptions: Even small obstructions cause major slowdowns
  • Thermal Throttling: Dish overheats in extreme heat (>122¬∞F)
  • Satellite Handoffs: Brief slowdowns during satellite switches
  • Network Deprioritization: Heavy users (1TB+) may be slowed
  • Beta Firmware: New updates sometimes reduce performance
  • Ground Station Distance: Farther stations increase latency

HughesNet/Viasat Specific Issues

  • Data Cap Throttling: Primary cause - speeds drop to 1-3 Mbps
  • Video Data Saver: Forces all video to 480p quality
  • Fair Access Policy: Additional throttling for "excessive" use
  • Beam Congestion: Too many users in your coverage beam
  • Bonus Zone Confusion: Full speeds only 2-8 AM
  • Plan Limitations: Lower tier plans have lower priority

Weather-Related Slowdowns

Weather significantly impacts satellite signals, especially during emergencies when storms are common.

Weather Impact Severity

  • Light Rain: 0-10% speed loss
  • Heavy Rain: 20-50% speed loss
  • Thunderstorms: 50-100% outage
  • Snow on Dish: 80-100% outage
  • Heavy Clouds: 5-15% speed loss
  • High Winds: Intermittent if dish moves

Weather Solutions

  • Rain: Wait it out, use rain fade resistant modulation
  • Snow: Brush off dish, use heater
  • Wind: Secure dish mounting
  • Heat: Shade dish, add cooling
  • Ice: De-icing spray safe for electronics
  • Clouds: No action needed usually

üí° Rain Fade Science

Rain fade occurs because water absorbs radio frequencies used by satellites. The effect is worse at higher frequencies: Ku-band (12-18 GHz) used by most satellites loses more signal than Ka-band (26-40 GHz). Starlink uses both but adapts automatically. Traditional satellites can't adapt, causing severe slowdowns.

Data Cap and Throttling Issues

For HughesNet and Viasat users, data caps are the primary cause of slow speeds.

Identifying Data Cap Throttling

  • Speed Test: Consistently 1-3 Mbps indicates throttling
  • Check Usage Meter: Provider app/website shows remaining data
  • Timing: Sudden slowdown mid-month suggests cap hit
  • All Devices Affected: Not just one computer/device
  • Time-Based: May improve 2-8 AM during bonus zone
‚ö†Ô∏è Data Cap Emergency: If you hit your cap during a disaster, options are limited: 1) Buy expensive data tokens ($10/GB), 2) Wait until 2 AM for bonus zone speeds, 3) Use mobile hotspot as backup, 4) Find public Wi-Fi if safe. This is why unlimited providers like Starlink are critical for emergency preparedness - caps don't exist.

Equipment Troubleshooting Steps

Systematic troubleshooting quickly identifies and resolves equipment-related slowdowns.

Quick Fix Sequence (15 minutes)

  1. Power Cycle: Unplug everything for 30 seconds
  2. Check Connections: Ensure all cables are secure
  3. Update Position: Dish may have shifted in wind
  4. Clear Snow/Debris: Clean dish surface gently
  5. Check App/Dashboard: Look for error messages
  6. Speed Test: Document current speeds
  7. Contact Support: If no improvement

Advanced Troubleshooting

  • Firmware Updates: Check for pending updates in app
  • Factory Reset: Last resort - reconfigure from scratch
  • Cable Inspection: Look for damage or water ingress
  • Router Logs: Check for error patterns
  • DNS Changes: Try Google DNS (8.8.8.8)
  • MTU Settings: Reduce if packet fragmentation occurring

When to Contact Support

Knowing when professional help is needed saves time during critical situations.

Contact Support When:

  • Hardware Damage: Physical damage to dish or cables
  • Account Issues: Billing or service suspension
  • Persistent Problems: Issues lasting over 24 hours
  • Error Messages: Specific error codes appearing
  • No Signal: Complete loss of connectivity
  • Unusual Behavior: Speeds far below plan minimums

Information to Provide Support

  • Speed test results with timestamps
  • Error messages or codes
  • Troubleshooting already attempted
  • Weather conditions at your location
  • Recent changes to setup

Slow Speed Questions

Why does my satellite internet slow down at night?
Network congestion peaks 5-11 PM when everyone streams video and games online. Traditional satellite providers also implement "network management" (throttling) during these hours. Starlink handles congestion better but may still slow slightly.
Can trees cause intermittent slowdowns?
Yes, even partial obstructions from tree branches cause significant slowdowns. As satellites move across the sky (Starlink) or Earth rotates (GEO satellites), trees block different signal paths throughout the day, causing variable speeds.
Why is upload speed much slower than download?
Satellite internet typically has asymmetric speeds by design. Upload requires your dish to transmit to space, requiring more power and facing more interference. Typical ratios are 10:1 (100 Mbps down, 10 Mbps up).
Does satellite internet get slower over time?
Equipment can degrade (dish alignment shifts, cables weather), and network congestion increases as more users join. However, new satellite launches and technology updates often improve speeds over time, especially with Starlink.
Why do speed tests show different results?
Different speed test servers, testing protocols, and network routes cause variations. Use the same test (fast.com or speedtest.net) consistently. Also, provider networks may prioritize speed test traffic, showing better results than actual usage.
