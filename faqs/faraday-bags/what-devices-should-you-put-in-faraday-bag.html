What devices should you put in a Faraday bag?

Priority devices for Faraday bags include smartphones, emergency radios, GPS units, car key fobs, backup power banks, and critical medical devices. Focus on electronics essential for communication, navigation, and survival during emergencies, plus devices vulnerable to tracking or electromagnetic interference.

What This Means

Device selection for Faraday protection should be strategic, focusing on electronics that serve critical functions during emergencies or require protection from tracking and interference. Not every electronic device needs Faraday protection - prioritize based on vulnerability and importance.

The goal is protecting devices that maintain functionality when you need them most: after electromagnetic events, during privacy-sensitive situations, or when preventing unauthorized access is crucial.

Consider both immediate needs (privacy protection, security) and long-term preparedness (emergency communication, navigation, power) when choosing which devices to protect.

How It Works (Step-by-Step)

Strategic device selection follows a priority-based approach:

  1. Assess your vulnerabilities: Identify which devices are most critical for your safety, communication, navigation, and security needs.
  2. Evaluate electromagnetic sensitivity: Determine which devices are most vulnerable to EMP, solar flares, or interference.
  3. Consider size and practicality: Choose devices that fit your available Faraday bag sizes and storage capabilities.
  4. Prioritize multi-function devices: Focus on electronics that serve multiple critical purposes rather than single-use items.
  5. Plan for scenarios: Select devices based on specific emergency, privacy, or security scenarios you're preparing for.

The most effective approach combines immediate protection needs with long-term emergency preparedness planning.

What It Protects (and What It Doesn't)

Essential Communication Devices (Top Priority)

  • Smartphones: Primary communication, navigation, emergency contacts, and information access
  • Two-way radios: Communication when cell networks fail, emergency coordination, family contact
  • Emergency radios: Weather alerts, news updates, emergency broadcasts during disasters
  • Satellite communicators: Emergency messaging when all other systems fail

Navigation and Location Devices

  • GPS units: Navigation when phone GPS fails, emergency location finding
  • Personal locator beacons: Emergency rescue signaling in remote areas
  • Car key fobs: Vehicle access and starting capability, especially newer electronic keys
  • Marine radios and GPS: For boats and maritime emergency communication

Power and Charging Equipment

  • Power banks and battery packs: Emergency power for critical devices
  • Solar chargers with electronics: Sustainable power generation with control circuits
  • Emergency flashlights: Especially LED models with electronic components
  • Hand-crank radios/chargers: Self-powered devices with sensitive circuits

Medical and Safety Devices

  • Medical monitoring devices: Glucose meters, blood pressure monitors, pulse oximeters
  • Hearing aids: Essential for communication and safety
  • Emergency beacons: Personal emergency notification devices
  • Medical alert devices: Life-critical monitoring and communication systems

Data and Information Storage

  • Backup hard drives: Critical data, documents, emergency information
  • USB drives and memory cards: Important files, software, emergency contacts
  • Tablets and e-readers: Information access, emergency manuals, maps
  • Laptops (if space allows): Communication, information processing, emergency coordination

Devices That Benefit Most

  • Battery-powered communication devices
  • Electronics with sensitive circuits
  • Devices you can't easily replace
  • Multi-function emergency equipment
  • Privacy-sensitive devices (phones, laptops)
  • Navigation and location equipment
  • Medical devices for health emergencies

Devices That Need Less Protection

  • Simple mechanical devices without circuits
  • Devices you can easily replace
  • Electronics you don't use for emergencies
  • Devices too large for practical storage
  • Equipment that needs constant connection
  • Low-value consumer electronics

Who This Is Best For

Device protection strategies work best for specific user groups:

Emergency preparedness planners who maintain backup systems for disasters. They prioritize communication devices, emergency radios, GPS units, and power equipment essential for post-disaster recovery and coordination.

Remote workers and travelers who depend on electronic devices for safety and communication in isolated areas. They focus on satellite communicators, backup phones, GPS devices, and portable power systems.

Privacy and security professionals who need to protect sensitive devices from tracking and unauthorized access. They prioritize phones, laptops, data storage devices, and communication equipment.

People with medical dependencies who rely on electronic medical devices for health monitoring or treatment. They prioritize medical equipment, hearing aids, monitoring devices, and emergency communication tools.

Vehicle owners with electronic systems who want backup access to cars with electronic keys, navigation systems, and communication equipment during electromagnetic events.

How to Test Device Protection Needs

Testing helps determine which devices actually need Faraday protection:

Critical function assessment:

  1. List all electronic devices you rely on for safety and communication
  2. Identify which devices would be impossible to replace quickly
  3. Determine which electronics serve multiple emergency functions
  4. Evaluate which devices contain sensitive or irreplaceable data
  5. Prioritize devices that work independently without infrastructure

Electromagnetic vulnerability test:

  1. Research which devices are most sensitive to electromagnetic interference
  2. Test devices in Faraday bags to ensure they fit and seal properly
  3. Verify that protected devices function normally when removed
  4. Confirm that backup devices have adequate battery life for emergencies
  5. Evaluate if storage affects device performance over time

Practical storage evaluation:

  1. Measure available Faraday bag space for different device sizes
  2. Test how long you can practically store devices without use
  3. Verify that multiple devices can be protected in available bags
  4. Evaluate if protection interferes with regular device maintenance
  5. Assess whether protected devices meet your actual emergency needs

Should I protect my everyday phone or get a backup phone for Faraday storage?

Most people benefit from a backup phone for Faraday storage rather than regularly bagging their primary device. This provides emergency communication without disrupting daily connectivity.

Do older devices need less Faraday protection than newer electronics?

Older devices with simpler circuits may be more resistant to some electromagnetic threats, but they still benefit from protection. Focus on devices you can't replace regardless of age.

How many devices can I fit in one Faraday bag?

This depends on bag size, but most people can fit a phone, small radio, GPS unit, and power bank in a medium-sized Faraday bag. Avoid overcrowding that prevents proper sealing.

Summary / Takeaway

Focus Faraday bag protection on devices essential for communication, navigation, emergency response, and privacy security. Prioritize smartphones, emergency radios, GPS units, car keys, medical devices, and backup power equipment over replaceable consumer electronics.

The most effective approach combines immediate protection needs (privacy, security) with emergency preparedness planning (communication, navigation, power). Choose devices that serve multiple functions and can't be easily replaced during emergencies.

Test device protection by evaluating critical functions, electromagnetic vulnerability, and practical storage requirements. Focus on electronics that maintain independence from infrastructure and provide essential capabilities when you need them most.
