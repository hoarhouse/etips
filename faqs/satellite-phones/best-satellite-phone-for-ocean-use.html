Coast Guard Communication Requirements

The U.S. Coast Guard requires commercial vessels operating beyond 20 miles from shore to carry emergency position indicating radio beacons (EPIRBs). While satellite phones aren't legally required, they provide crucial two-way communication that EPIRBs cannot offer, enabling detailed emergency coordination and routine safety check-ins.

Ocean Emergency Communication Best Practices

Emergency Preparation

  • Register with Coast Guard: File float plans and emergency contact information
  • Program Emergency Numbers: Coast Guard, Marine Police, and emergency contacts
  • Test Before Departure: Verify satellite phone functionality before leaving port
  • Backup Power: Carry solar chargers and backup batteries for extended voyages
  • Waterproof Storage: Use waterproof cases and protective storage solutions

Emergency Communication Protocol

  1. Mayday Call: Use VHF Channel 16 first if within coastal range
  2. Satellite Phone Backup: Use satellite phone if VHF fails or out of range
  3. GPS Coordinates: Immediately provide precise location coordinates
  4. Vessel Description: Provide vessel name, size, color, and distinguishing features
  5. Emergency Details: Describe emergency type, number of people, and immediate needs
  6. Stay Connected: Maintain communication with rescue services until help arrives

Routine Communication

  • Daily Check-ins: Establish regular contact schedules with shore support
  • Position Reports: Share location and status updates regularly
  • Weather Updates: Request current weather and forecast information
  • Port Communications: Coordinate arrival times and customs procedures

Additional Ocean Communication Equipment

Complementary Marine Communication Systems

  • VHF Marine Radio: Primary short-range communication with other vessels and coast guard
  • EPIRB (Emergency Position Indicating Radio Beacon): Automatic distress beacon for life-threatening emergencies
  • AIS (Automatic Identification System): Vessel tracking and collision avoidance system
  • SSB Radio: Long-range radio communication for extended ocean voyages
  • Personal Locator Beacon (PLB): Individual emergency beacons for crew overboard situations

Why Satellite Phones Are Essential

While other marine communication systems serve specific purposes, satellite phones provide the only reliable two-way voice communication capability that works globally in all ocean conditions. This makes them indispensable for complex emergency coordination, medical consultations, and routine communication with shore support during extended ocean voyages.
