What is the best satellite phone to buy?

For life-saving emergency communication, the Iridium Extreme PTT offers the most reliable global coverage, working everywhere including polar regions. For disaster preparedness in populated areas, the Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 provides excellent value with 90% global coverage at lower monthly costs. Both have proven themselves in real disasters.

What This Means

Choosing a satellite phone isn't like picking a smartphone. There are only a handful of serious options, and they're all quite different from each other.

The "best" phone depends on where you'll use it, how often, and what features matter most to you. Some excel in extreme conditions, others offer better value for occasional use.

Don't worry about getting overwhelmed by choices. There are really only 3-4 phones worth considering, and each has clear strengths that make the decision easier once you know what you need.

How It Works

Here's how to choose the right satellite phone:

  1. Decide your coverage area - Global vs regional
  2. Set your budget - Phone cost plus monthly service
  3. Consider durability needs - Rugged vs standard
  4. Pick your network - Iridium, Inmarsat, or Globalstar
  5. Choose your phone - Based on network and features

Most people start with coverage area. If you need true global coverage including poles, you want Iridium. If you stay in populated areas, other networks work fine and cost less.

Where It Works and Where It Doesn't

2025 Emergency Preparedness Recommendations:

  • Best for Life-Safety: Iridium Extreme PTT - Works everywhere, $1,599 device + $72-120/month
  • Best Value for Disasters: Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 - $1,080 device + $35-50/month starting
  • Most Reliable: Iridium Extreme PTT - Proven in 2025 California wildfires
  • Best for Budget Emergency Plans: Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 with prepaid units
  • Easiest Emergency Use: Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 - Simple interface, GPS SOS

Avoid these situations:

  • Buying based on price alone
  • Ignoring coverage maps for your area
  • Not factoring in monthly service costs
  • Choosing a phone that's overkill for your needs

Remember: the "best" phone is the one that works where you need it and fits your budget. An expensive global phone is wasteful if you only travel regionally.

Pros and Cons

❌ Iridium Phones

Who This Is Best For

Iridium Extreme PTT is perfect for:

  • Professionals who need reliable global communication
  • Expedition leaders and extreme adventurers
  • Maritime workers on international routes
  • Emergency responders and military personnel
  • Anyone going to polar regions

Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 is great for:

  • Recreational users who want backup communication
  • Regional travelers (not polar)
  • Budget-conscious buyers
  • First-time satellite phone users
  • Occasional emergency use

Most people are better served by the IsatPhone 2 unless they specifically need global coverage or extreme durability. It's easier to use and much more affordable.

Mini-FAQ

How much should I expect to spend in 2025?

Budget $1,080-1,599 for the device plus monthly service: Iridium $72-120/month (15-30 minutes), Inmarsat $35-50/month starting. Prepaid options: Iridium $102.50 for 75 minutes, Inmarsat 1,000 units for $1,150.

Can I buy a used satellite phone?

Yes, but be careful. Make sure it's not blacklisted, includes all accessories, and can be activated on current plans. Used phones save money but may lack warranty coverage.

What about rental options?

Many companies rent satellite phones for short trips. This makes sense for occasional use or trying before buying. Expect $50-100/week plus usage charges.

Summary / Takeaway

The best satellite phone is the one that works where you need it most. For true global coverage and maximum reliability, choose Iridium. For regional use and better value, consider Inmarsat.

Don't overthink it. There are only a few good options, and they're all proven devices used by professionals worldwide. Pick based on your coverage area and budget, and you'll be happy with any of the major brands.

Remember that the monthly service cost often exceeds the phone cost over time. Factor this into your decision - sometimes paying more upfront for a better network saves money in the long run.
