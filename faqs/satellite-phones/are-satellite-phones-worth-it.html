Are satellite phones worth it?

For most people, satellite phones are worth it if you regularly go to remote areas, work in emergency services, or live somewhere with unreliable cell coverage. They're not worth it if you rarely leave areas with good cell service and are just buying for "someday" scenarios.

What This Means

The value of a satellite phone depends entirely on how likely you are to actually need it. They're expensive tools that excel in specific situations.

Think of it like a generator. If you live in an area with frequent power outages, a generator is absolutely worth it. But if your power is reliable, spending thousands on a generator you'll never use doesn't make sense.

Satellite phones are similar. For some people, they're essential safety equipment. For others, they're an expensive gadget that sits in a drawer.

How It Works

Here's how to determine if a satellite phone is worth it for you:

  1. Calculate your real costs - Device ($1,080-1,599) + service (Iridium $72-120/month, Inmarsat $35-50/month)
  2. Assess your risk - How often are you in areas with no cell coverage?
  3. Consider alternatives - Satellite messengers, PLBs, or rental options
  4. Factor in peace of mind - What's communication worth to you?
  5. Think long-term - Will you actually use it over 3-5 years?

Be honest about your actual usage. Many people buy satellite phones thinking they'll use them more than they actually do.

Where It Works and Where It Doesn't

Absolutely worth it if you:

  • Live in wildfire, hurricane, or earthquake zones
  • Work in remote areas where accidents happen
  • Have family members with medical conditions
  • Are responsible for others' safety (guides, pilots, boat captains)
  • Live in areas with frequent power outages
  • Travel to remote locations for work or recreation

Probably not worth it if you:

  • Rarely leave areas with cell coverage
  • Only want it for theoretical emergencies
  • Can't afford the ongoing monthly costs
  • Have never been in a situation where you needed one
  • Could use cheaper alternatives like satellite messengers

The key question: In the past year, how many times were you somewhere with no cell coverage where communication would have been valuable?

Pros and Cons

✅ Worth It When

  • You regularly need remote communication
  • Your safety depends on staying connected
  • You can afford the ongoing costs
  • You work in high-risk environments
  • Peace of mind is valuable to you
  • You have specific emergency protocols

❌ Not Worth It When

  • You have good cell coverage 95% of the time
  • The cost strains your budget
  • You're buying for unlikely scenarios
  • You won't maintain the service plan
  • Cheaper alternatives meet your needs
  • You're an infrequent user

Who This Is Best For

High-value users (definitely worth it):

  • Professional guides and outdoor instructors
  • Maritime workers and commercial fishermen
  • Remote construction and mining workers
  • Emergency responders and search and rescue
  • Military and security contractors
  • Journalists in remote or dangerous areas

Moderate-value users (consider carefully):

  • Serious hikers and mountaineers
  • Rural residents with poor cell coverage
  • People in hurricane/earthquake zones
  • Frequent international travelers
  • Boat owners who go offshore

If you're not in one of these categories, consider starting with a satellite messenger or emergency beacon instead. They're much cheaper and might meet your actual needs.

Mini-FAQ

What's the total cost over 3 years in 2025?

Budget $3,340-5,919 total: Iridium ($1,599 + $2,592-4,320 service) or Inmarsat ($1,080 + $1,260-1,800 service). For life-saving capability during disasters, many consider this invaluable.

Are there cheaper alternatives?

Yes! Satellite messengers ($300-400) and personal locator beacons ($300) offer emergency communication for much less. They don't do voice calls but handle emergencies.

Can I try before buying?

Many companies rent satellite phones for $50-100/week. This is perfect for testing whether you'd actually use one regularly before buying.

Summary / Takeaway

Satellite phones are worth it if you regularly put yourself in situations where you need reliable communication and can't count on cell service. They're not worth it for theoretical "what if" scenarios.

Be realistic about your usage. If you haven't been in a situation in the past year where you wished you had a satellite phone, you probably don't need one. Consider cheaper alternatives like satellite messengers first.

For those who truly need them - guides, remote workers, emergency responders - satellite phones aren't just worth it, they're essential. The peace of mind and safety they provide far outweighs the cost.
